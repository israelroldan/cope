# Online K‑12 Soft Skills Education: Market & Strategic Positioning Report

# Scope of “Future Skills” Education for Kids (Ages 6–18)
**Focus of Analysis:** This report examines online education companies that specialize in “soft skills,” study skills, and 21st-century skills for children and teens (roughly ages 6 to 18). These skills include communication (public speaking, debate, storytelling), collaboration and leadership, critical thinking and problem-solving, creativity and innovation, emotional intelligence, and meta-learning (time management, organization, learning how to learn). Unlike traditional academic tutoring, these programs emphasize personal development and “future-ready” competencies that schools often overlook.**Delivery Modality:** All profiled programs deliver learning primarily online. Formats range from live 1:1 coaching and small-group classes to cohort-based courses or structured self-paced modules. Many integrate live interactive sessions (via proprietary platforms or Zoom) with engaging activities, projects, or games to keep kids interested.**Key Players Identified:** The landscape is diverse, including dedicated soft-skills startups and broader ed-tech platforms. Major examples include:

PlanetSpark (India-origin, global) – Specializes in communication skills (public speaking, creative writing) for 

KidsKey (Cyprus-based, global Russian diaspora focus) – Online school with soft-skills and language courses for bilingual

Outschool (US-based marketplace) – A platform hosting thousands of classes by independent teachers, including many in soft skills (public speaking clubs, study skills workshops, etc.)

**Regional and Emerging Players:** In Europe and Latin America, the market is nascent. For example, KidsKey caters to European bilingual families, and UpSoftSkills (Spanish-language) has begun offering soft-skills courses for niños (children) in Latin A. However, much of the content is still delivered in English, indicating an opportunity for localized offerings.

For each of the main companies above, we profile their skill focus, age targets, and customer model (B2C vs B2B) before analyzing their strategies in depth.
**Age Ranges & Segments:** Most providers group offerings by age or grade:

Young children (approx 6–10): Programs often emphasize basic communication, confidence building, social skills, and creativity through playful learning.

Pre-teens (10–13): Often targeted for more structured skill building – e. introductory public speaking, teamwork exercises, study habits.

Teens (14–18): Advanced programs focusing on leadership, interview skills, debate, entrepreneurship, and academic strategies (note-taking, exam prep techniques).

Business Model (B2C vs B2B): The majority sell direct-to-consumer, i. to parents as an enrichment or tutoring service. A few explore B2B/education partnerships (offering their curriculum to schools or learning centers), but this is less common. **Several macro-level factors are propelling this market expansion:** 

**Increased Screen Time & Remote Learning:** Kids today spend more time online and grew accustomed to learning via screens during 2020-21. This normalized the idea of online coaching for non-academic subjects. Parents observed that kids can meaningfully socialize and learn skills online. Also, many students struggled with purely online academics, highlighting the need for better self-regulation and engagement skills. As a result, there’s interest in programs that teach children how to thrive in a digital learning environment (e.g., classes on remote learning success as seen on O). Plus, with screen time up, parents prefer it to be productive screen time – an online soft-skills class is seen as a constructive use of digital hours.

**Parent Anxiety around Academic Performance and Future Employability:** Even before the pandemic, parental concern about their children’s competitive edge was high. Now, with learning disruptions, many worry their kids are “falling behind” academically or lacking motivation and focus. Additionally, looking ahead 10-15 years, parents fret about college admissions and employability in a rapidly changing world. Soft skills are increasingly seen as the differentiator. For example, in a highly automated future, “people skills are likely to become even more in demand by companies as AI replaces some... work” Parents internalize messages that good grades alone aren’t enough; children also need creativity, communication, leadership to succeed. This drives them to seek supplemental training in those areas, akin to how previous generations sought music or sports lessons to build character. Essentially, soft skills classes are becoming the new “must-have” enrichment, much like coding classes were a few years ago.

**AI and Automation Emphasizing Human Skills:** The rise of AI is a big narrative. Thought leaders and media emphasize that skills like empathy, collaboration, and adaptability will be what humans need to compete and collaborate with AI. An education expert noted that schools must teach skills “machines can’t replicate” so kids can compete in a workforce with AI. This includes “ability to collaborate, monitor one’s own emotions and learning (metacognitive skills), interpersonal skills” Such commentary (often seen on news and LinkedIn) filters down to parents, creating a sense of urgency to cultivate these uniquely human skills early. It’s a defensive play: if AI might handle coding or calculation in the future, what will set my child apart? Likely their creativity, leadership, and emotional intelligence. Therefore, classes in public speaking, creative thinking, teamwork exercises, etc., are viewed as an investment in future-proofing a child’s career. Even at government and school levels, there’s increased focus on 21st-century skills (e.g., OECD’s Education 2030 framework calls for integrating these skills in c). But as long as schools lag in implementation, private programs fill the gap.

**Post-Pandemic Normalization of Live Online Coaching:** Pre-2020, online lessons for kids (especially young kids) had skepticism. Post-2020, families have seen it work and even offer advantages. Now, parents are more willing to seek specialized help online for their children’s needs beyond the local area. In fact, experts say families are “increasingly willing to go outside of school… [due to] the ease of education technology” and desire for  Virtual extracurriculars are no longer odd; they’re part of the ecosystem. This has two effects: (1) Market Expansion – more customers available and comfortable with the medium. (2) Global Competition – providers from anywhere can serve a child, meaning a family in Europe can hire an Indian tutor for soft skills, etc., broadening choices. The pandemic also directly created needs: children missed social interaction practice and fell into disorganized study habits, so parents turned to programs to rebuild those soft skills. The concept of a “coach” or “mentor” for soft skills gained traction (similar to how adults hire life coaches, now kids might have a communication coach). As evidence of normalization, consider that hundreds of Outschool teachers earned six-figure incomes in 2020 by teaching niche classes o, something unimaginable pre-pandemic. And mainstream education media continue to discuss online tutoring and coaching as a standard part of education recovery 

**Globalization and Cross-Cultural Interaction:** The world is more interconnected for today’s youth (through the internet, gaming, international schools, etc.). This creates demand for skills like cross-cultural communication, collaboration with peers globally, and multilingual soft skills. For example, KidsKey’s success is partly due to globalization – kids moving countries and needing to maintain communication skills in multiple languages. Similarly, parents see value in children engaging with international peers (as HOSS touts “global exposure” in c). As global opportunities (and competition) increase, families want their kids to be comfortable communicating beyond their local context, which fuels interest in broad soft skills (from etiquette in diverse settings to public speaking that can reach international audiences).

In summary, socio-technical trends and recent events have both heightened awareness of soft skills’ importance and broken down barriers to accessing training for those skills, fueling rapid growth in this education segment.
Competitive Landscape Mapping
**The competitive landscape can be thought of in layers and niches:** 

Pure-Play Soft Skills / Communication Specialists: These are companies whose primary focus is soft skills for kids. Examples: PlanetSpark, House of Soft Skills (HOSS), KidsKey (partially), PlanetEdu (hypothetical) etc. They differentiate by deep curriculum in these areas. Many are startups (mostly under 5–6 years old), often founded in India or the U.S., but targeting global markets. They compete on who can deliver better outcomes in confidence and communication. We see multiple entrants, especially in public speaking for kids, which appears quite crowded (PlanetSpark, HOSS, 98thPercentile, Storytellers Institute, and smaller regional ones all vying for similar customers). Within this, some specialize further: e.g., a company might focus only on debate for kids, or only on social skills for neurodiverse kids (though those tend to be smaller outfits or individual practitioners). Differentiation factors include curriculum quality, live 1:1 vs group model, pricing, and cultural targeting (Western markets vs Asian, etc.). Many are in a land-grab for English-speaking students worldwide, often using similar online marketing channels. No single brand dominates globally yet, though PlanetSpark has a strong presence due to aggressive funding and marketing. It’s worth noting some offline institutions historically in this space: e.g., Toastmasters Youth Leadership, Helen O’Grady Drama Academy, local public speaking clubs, etc., but these are now either moving online or partnering with online players to stay relevant.

**General Edtech Platforms Bundling Soft Skills:** This includes companies primarily known for academic or other enrichment that have added soft skills as one category. 98thPercentile is one example, adding public speaking to their math/ELA lineup. Another is BYJU’s (the giant Indian edtech) – primarily academic, but through WhiteHat Jr they offered coding plus a bit of “math plus music” etc., and I believe they also considered creative courses. Vedantu (another Indian tutoring co) launched a “SuperSkills” series in 2021 to cover skills like reading and coding, which might extend to soft skills. Udemy and Coursera even have courses for teens on soft skills (less live, more on-demand). Additionally, GoStudent (tutoring platform in Europe) could potentially include study skills coaching by their tutors. These generalists have existing user bases and can cross-sell soft skills classes. They might not have as specialized content as pure-plays, but they have reach. For example, a parent already using an online math tutor might take an add-on package for “learning skills coaching.” Also, school-oriented edtech (like Prodigy, etc.) primarily focus on academics, but we see some slowly integrating SEL content into their platforms, indirectly addressing soft skills.

Marketplaces (Aggregation of independent offerings): Outschool is the prime example, essentially aggregating many “micro-competitors” (individual teachers or small outfits). Others similar: Allschool (a newer marketplace with emphasis on Asian markets), KidPass (which moved online during the pandemic), and some local marketplace startups in various countries. These platforms compete by breadth and convenience rather than by unified pedagogy. They are strong in fragmented markets – if no dominant brand exists, marketplaces thrive by offering variety. Right now, with soft skills being broad (public speaking, study skills, EQ, etc.), marketplaces can capture those looking for very specific or niche solutions (e.g., “speech class for 10-year-old homeschooler who loves Minecraft” could find a tailored Outschool class). Marketplaces also empower offline coaches to come online easily. For instance, a school speech & debate coach who lost work during the pandemic could start a class on Outschool and effectively become a competitor to PlanetSpark on a smaller scale. This leads to a highly fragmented competitive field – countless small providers, which collectively serve many, but each only a tiny share.

**Offline Traditional Players moving online:** Think of local public speaking institutes (like a community center youth oratory class, or private tuition centers offering “Personality development” – common in South Asia), summer camps for debate or leadership, and educational companies like Dale Carnegie (Youth Programs) or 4-H, Scouting organizations which often teach soft skills (leadership, public speaking) as part of their youth development. During 2020, many had to move online; some discovered a new model. For instance, debate clubs that used to meet in person started doing Zoom debates, and some opened these up to kids outside their region. Dale Carnegie launched live online classes for teens on public speaking and people skills (since their physical workshops were halted). These offline-origin competitors have strong pedagogy and reputation, but often less tech-savvy. As things reopen, many are going back to physical or hybrid. However, they remain competitors because parents might choose a local in-person program instead of an online one, especially for soft skills where interpersonal interaction is key. The question is whether offline players will expand online aggressively (some might, seeing an opportunity to scale beyond their locale). So far, none of the global offline brands (e.g., Dale Carnegie, Toastmasters) have fully capitalized on online youth training at scale – leaving room for online-first companies to dominate. But regionally, one may find small businesses (e.g., a speech coach or a drama instructor) who now offer Zoom classes, effectively competing for the same students as the startups, often at lower price (since overhead is lower and marketing is local word-of-mouth).

**Content Publishers and Apps:** There’s an emerging segment of apps or digital products focusing on SEL and soft skills. For example, apps for kids that teach emotional regulation through games, or time-management apps for students (like habit trackers). While not direct “classes,” they compete for attention and claim to improve certain skills. Some schools use SEL software (like Mightifier or ClassDojo’s SEL modules). If parents rely on these or school-provided programs, they might be less inclined to pay for a separate course. However, these apps often complement rather than replace coaching.

**Fragmentation vs Concentration:** Currently, the market is highly fragmented. There is no single dominant global brand for K-12 soft skills akin to how maybe Khan Academy is for free academic resources or Duolingo for language learning. PlanetSpark aspires to that leadership in communication, but it faces many competitors and is not yet a household name globally. Outschool is a big name but is a marketplace, not a branded curriculum – its strength is scale, not singular content focus. The fragmentation is along several lines:

Geography/Language: Many providers target specific regions or languages (KidsKey for Russian speakers, UpSoftSkills for Spanish speakers, etc.). This means in each region, top players differ. For instance, in India, PlanetSpark and HOSS are prominent; in the U.S., Outschool and 98thPercentile; in Latin America, perhaps a local startup like Talently (just hypothetical) might appear; in Europe, some local public speaking coaches or UK-based programs might be better known.

**Skill sub-niches:** Some niches are crowded – notably public speaking for kids. This is a very popular entry point because it’s tangible and many parents recognize it as a need. So we see several companies and countless individuals offering this. Creative writing for kids is another overlapping area (PlanetSpark does it, as do many writing-focused programs). Debate training (e.g., organizations like Debate Mate in the UK, or online debate leagues) is somewhat crowded among older kids. On the other hand, some sub-niches are underserved or relatively new:

Study skills/executive function for kids – there are a few specialists (some education therapists or small outfits focusing on ADHD coaching), but no major scaled player purely for study skills. Many kids could benefit from “learning how to learn” courses, yet few comprehensive programs exist – a gap in concentration.

Social skills for special needs – some presence on Outschool and local therapists, but not a mainstream offering from the bigger edtechs.

Creativity/Innovation – beyond generic “creative thinking” activities, not many structured programs purely on creativity for kids (aside from maybe robotics or coding classes that tangentially do it). A platform specifically for creative skill-building is not prominent.

Global citizenship or cross-cultural collaboration – aside from exchange programs or Model UN clubs, not many courses teach kids how to work in cross-cultural teams or communicate across cultures; this could be an emerging niche as the world globalizes.

Emotional intelligence & resilience – typically covered in therapy or SEL contexts, but not many private group classes focus solely on EQ (some might include it within soft skills, like HOSS does mention empathy and emotional IQ in passing). So one could say that space isn’t yet crowded.

**Concentration of a few large players:** We do see some concentration in broad categories: Outschool in marketplace, PlanetSpark in communication (with significant funding), and a handful of others. But compared to say the coding-for-kids market (where a few giants emerged), the soft skills market remains more open. One reason is that soft skills are multifaceted and culturally nuanced; a one-size-fits-all approach is harder. Also, network effects are less – e.g., coding benefited from large curriculum development and marketing, whereas soft skills success often depends on the personal quality of instructors and word-of-mouth, which can favor smaller, agile providers.

**Regional vs Global Brands:** It appears no single global brand yet dominates. PlanetSpark is attempting to be one (reaching various continents)s, and Outschool is global (though mainly English-speaking). But regionally:

India/South Asia: A lot of activity. Players like PlanetSpark, HOSS, 98thPercentile (though U.S.-based, Indian-run), and others such as LEAD School or Camp K12 (Camp K12 started with coding but did creativity workshops and could pivot) are active. There’s competition to capture not just Indian market but use India as a base to serve abroad (as PlanetSpark and HOSS do).

**North America:** Outschool, individual providers, and possibly tutoring companies expanding services. Also specialized camps (e.g., Acton Academy’s Children’s Business Fair or speaking programs). But no dominating corporate player specifically for soft skills; much is fragmented across local debate clubs, etc., aside from Outschool aggregating them.

**Europe:** More fragmented by country/language. Some traditional organizations (like youth theatres, debate clubs). Online, a venture like KidsKey addresses a specific need. Possibly local startups like SkillsBooster (hypothetical) in various countries could exist but haven’t attained global visibility. European parents might also tap into U.S./global platforms if English is acceptable, or use whatever local language programs exist.

**Latin America:** Very early-stage. The presence of a site like UpSoftS suggests emerging interest. Spanish-language edtech in this space may grow soon. Currently, many Latin American parents who want such training may use English programs or rely on schools that incorporate “habilidades blandas” (some private schools in LATAM emphasize this internally).

China/East Asia: Not in scope explicitly due to language barrier, but worth noting: Chinese edtechs post-2021 regulations refocused on non-academic courses. Soft skills like speaking and leadership could be areas they explore for “quality education.” For instance, Zuoyebang or others might launch leadership camps (in Chinese). But given regulations, they’d be careful. Some international-facing Chinese companies might offer classes in English globally too. However, due to the user’s focus (Europe/LatAm), this is peripheral.

**Summary:** The competitive environment is lively, with no monopoly. Parents have many options: from polished platforms to indie tutors. The challenge for any new entrant is to find a distinct segment or superior method to stand out among this fragmentation, or conversely to aggregate offerings in a compelling way.**Certain categories are crowded:** 

**Public speaking:** Many choices; one must differentiate perhaps by age focus, quality (e.g., PlanetSpark 1:1 vs cheaper group classes), or by integrating with contests (some companies host speech competitions as a feature).

Generic “personality development”: somewhat saturated in marketing (lots of claims of confidence building), so credibility and specialization matter.

**Others are underserved:** 

**Study skills for neurodivergent or even mainstream kids:** few scaled programs.

Soft skills for very young children (Kindergarten to Grade 2): aside from social skills playgroups, not much, since attention spans are short; but potentially an area if done via games with parent involvement.

Specialized soft skills like “Kids’ Emotional Intelligence coaching”: typically left to school counselors or not addressed early; a startup could focus there with psychology-backed methods.

Innovation in content delivery (like VR public speaking training for teens to simulate stage presence – something not mainstream yet, but conceivable as an edge).

# Gaps, Underserved Needs, and Opportunities
Analyzing the above, several gaps in the current offerings emerge. These represent opportunities for a new venture to serve unmet needs or differentiate itself:
(1) Underserved Learner Personas:

Older Teens (16–18) Preparing for Adulthood: Most programs target younger kids or early teens. High school juniors/seniors have specific soft-skill needs (college interviews, personal statements, job/internship interviews, stress management for exams). This age group might not be fully served by kid-focused platforms. An offering tailored to, say, 17-year-olds prepping for university or work (covering interview skills, resume building, networking, time management for self-study, etc.) could fill a gap. Currently, they might attend occasional school workshops or figure it out themselves. A focused “soft skills bootcamp for college & career” could resonate with both teens and parents (and even schools).

Early Primary (ages 5–7): There are fewer structured soft-skills programs for very young children outside of general socialization (kindergarten focuses on sharing, etc.). However, early intervention can be valuable – e.g., building communication confidence at a young age, or teaching simple self-regulation techniques. KidsKey touches this with playful learning for 4-7 in language, but an opportunity exists for a program like “Play-based social skills and learning habits for early primary.” Parents of shy or hyperactive young kids might seek help in a fun format (short sessions with parent involvement perhaps, since at that age parental guidance is key). This segment is currently more served by in-person playgroups or therapy if extreme (like speech therapy), but not much in structured online form.

Shy/Introverted Kids Across Ages: While many programs claim to help shy kids, few specialize deeply in it. There’s an opportunity for a program that specifically tailors methods for introverts (maybe smaller groups, slower pace, using interests like gaming or art to coax them out). The marketing could directly address parents of shy children with empathy, rather than lumping them into generic confidence courses. A “Confidence for Quiet Kids” approach could differentiate. On Outschool, some classes explicitly mention i, showing demand.

Neurodivergent Learners (ADHD, ASD, dyslexia) Needing Executive Function and Social Skills: This is a major underserved persona. Children with ADHD often struggle with study skills (organization, time management) and those with ASD might need support with social communication. While therapy covers some, an edtech program focusing on Executive Function coaching for ADHD students or social skills for autistic teens could meet a huge need. Many parents pay for 1:1 executive function coaches or social groups, but an accessible, structured group program (with trained facilitators, maybe shorter sessions and visual aids for neurodiverse learners) is not widely seen online. A venture here can specialize by understanding the cognitive profiles and adapting methods (e.g., game-based goal-setting for ADHD, role-play with predictable routines for ASD, etc.). It’s a differentiated market (neurodivergent segment is often willing to pay for specialized help) with relatively few scaled competitors (mostly offline therapists or small Zoom groups run by non-profits).

**Gifted but Disorganized or Anxious Kids:** Another persona: academically bright students who underperform due to poor study habits or test anxiety. They might not be reached by general tutoring because content isn’t the issue. A program focusing on “Study skills and resilience for gifted students” or those in rigorous programs (like IB, A-levels) could be valuable. This could include advanced note-taking, coping with perfectionism, maintaining motivation, etc. Currently these kids either manage on their own or get advice from school counselors (which may not be enough). Such a program could partner with gifted associations or schools. It overlaps with executive function but specifically for high-achievers who need efficiency and stress management.

Children of Immigrants / Multilingual Families: Kids growing up in one culture but with parents from another often face unique challenges – e.g., accent or confidence issues speaking a non-native language, or cultural differences in communication style. KidsKey identified Russian bilinguals as one such niche. There are many other communities (e.g., Spanish-speaking family in the US whose child is shy in English at school, or Asian-immigrant families wanting kids to adopt more Western presentation styles while preserving their heritage language). A program that is culturally sensitive and perhaps bilingual could serve these. For example, “English public speaking for ESL kids” or “Soft skills for newcomers” could help immigrant children catch up on soft skills that local peers acquired. Given increasing migration, this is a growing need.

**Non-English Speaking Markets in Native Language:** Most online soft-skills courses are in English (targeting either native speakers or requiring English proficiency). This leaves a gap in huge markets: e.g., a Brazilian parent might want a Portuguese-language program on creativity for their child, a French parent might want a public speaking class in French. Aside from a few local providers (which may not have the polish or funding of bigger startups), not much exists. For instance, UpSoftSkills in Spanish is one example attempting to fill Spanish  A venture could choose a specific language market (Spanish, French, German, Dutch, etc.) and localize proven content into that language with cultural context. Europe in particular, with many languages and high value on communication, might be fertile ground – e.g., soft skills courses tailored to Dutch high school students (linking to things like their VWO or Gymnasium academic track where students often face heavy workload and stress – a resilience/study skills angle could tie in). Because English programs might not resonate fully or might be inaccessible to those who aren’t bilingual, this localization is an opening.

(2) Under-addressed Problem Definitions:

Executive Function (Planning, Focus, Procrastination): While some study skills courses touch on this, executive function coaching is mostly in the domain of specialized therapists or expensive 1:1 coaches. There’s a lack of fun, group-based approaches to teaching planning and focus to kids. For example, a “Project Management for Kids” or “Beat Procrastination Bootcamp” could teach through gamified tasks how to break tasks into steps, manage a schedule, and use tools (planners, apps) effectively. Given how many students struggle with organization (especially after periods of remote/hybrid school), this is a pain point. A program that makes it concrete – using visual planners, perhaps a game where they level up by completing tasks on time – could be innovative. It can especially target those moving to more independent schooling (transition to middle or high school). Parents of kids who habitually procrastinate or forget assignments would jump at a structured intervention. We haven’t seen a major kid-focused EF product in edtech yet, so this remains open.

Emotional Regulation, Stress & Anxiety around School: SEL curriculums exist in schools but often generic. A private program focusing on managing school-related stress, test anxiety, and building emotional resilience is underdeveloped. Many kids face high pressure (from competitive academics or personal perfectionism) and end up disengaging or burning out. A specialized course that teaches techniques like mindfulness, cognitive reframing, and coping strategies, but in a kid-friendly, interactive way (not just meditation apps), could fill a need. Possibly combine it with study skills for a holistic “academic resilience” program. For instance, something like “Exam Ninja: Stress Management and Study Hacks for Teens” – blending practical study methods with stress-reduction techniques. Currently, addressing this often requires going to counselors or using general mindfulness apps. A tailored program could differentiate by directly tackling exam anxiety or motivation slumps.

**Cross-Cultural Communication & Global Collaboration:** As noted, not many programs teach kids explicitly how to work with peers from different cultures. With increasing virtual exchange (even online gaming or collaborations), this could be valuable. A course might involve students from different countries working on small projects, learning about each other’s communication norms, maybe basic conflict resolution and empathy in diverse teams. It could be like a “global learners club” where they solve global problems (Model UN style or design challenges) – developing collaboration, leadership, and cultural intelligence. While organizations like AFS or CISV handle this through exchanges or camps, an online equivalent is an opportunity. The unique selling point: prepare your child for the global workplace by building cross-cultural teamwork skills early. Parents in internationally minded communities or with expat lifestyles would appreciate this.

Digital Citizenship, Online Safety, & AI Literacy Combined with Soft Skills: Digital citizenship (safe and responsible online behavior) is often taught in bits by schools or through PSAs, but not in an integrated skills way. There’s an opportunity for a program that covers how to communicate and behave in digital environments (e.g., etiquette in Zoom classes, understanding online tone, dealing with cyberbullying) alongside building positive digital habits (time management with devices, discerning real vs fake info – critical thinking). Additionally, AI literacy is emerging – teaching kids about AI (like how to interact with AI tools, their limitations, ethical issues). Merging AI literacy with soft skills could create something novel: for example, teaching a child how to use AI (say ChatGPT) as a tool for brainstorming a speech, but also emphasizing that human creativity and judgment are still needed. Or discussing the ethics of communication with AI-generated content – a very 21st-century topic. A course titled “Digital Age Skills: Communicate, Collaborate, and Stay Safe Online” could attract parents who worry about their kids’ online interactions and future readiness. It differentiates by acknowledging that part of communication today is digital (text, social media) – and kids need soft skills there too (like interpreting tone in text, managing one’s online persona). No major competitor is packaging these together yet.

(3) Promising but Underused Delivery Formats:

**Small Peer Cohorts with Long-Term Mentorship:** Many current programs are either short-term courses or one-off coaching. There’s an opportunity in a model where a small group of peers (4-6 students) stays together over a longer period (say a school year) with the same mentor, forming a tight-knit learning community. This mimics a “club” or even something like scouting, but online and skill-focused. The consistent mentor can personalize guidance, and the peer group can provide social motivation and a safe practice environment over time. For example, a cohort of 5 middle-schoolers who meet weekly for 9 months working on various soft skills – at the end, they have a deep camaraderie and have grown together. This long-term format builds trust, which is crucial for soft skills growth (students may open up more and challenge themselves in a stable group). It’s underused likely due to scheduling challenges and companies preferring scalable rotating enrollment. But for differentiation, one could market this as “Your child’s personal growth tribe” where each kid gets mentorship and peer support. It combines the advantages of 1:1 (personalization) and group (collaboration, friendships) with an added dimension of time for real growth. It could be especially effective for shy kids (they get comfortable with the group and gradually blossom).

**Parent-Child Joint Sessions:** Soft skills development can be reinforced by parent involvement, but few programs bring them together in the learning process. Imagine a format where occasionally, parents and kids attend a session together – for instance, a communication workshop where they practice active listening with each other, or a study habits session where parent and child jointly create a plan for homework routines. This could address the issue that often, parent expectations and child habits clash. By involving parents, you ensure continuity at home. It’s promising especially for younger kids or for study skills. E.g., a “Family Study Skills Training” might teach both the parent and child strategies, so the parent can coach at home in line with the program. Or an “Emotional intelligence for families” where both learn a common language for emotions. This is underused because many parents just enroll and step back; yet parents often express they wish they knew how to help. A venture using this format can differentiate with the message: “We don’t just train your child – we empower you as a parent to support their growth.” It creates a partnership. Even occasional joint sessions (like a parent joins every 4th class) could be enough. This is an area offline classes sometimes did via parent showcase nights or sending materials home; online, one could formalize it.

Asynchronous Micro-Lessons + Weekly Live Coaching (Blended): Most offerings are either fully live or fully self-paced. A blended approach could leverage the best of both. For instance, a platform might provide short 5-10 minute video lessons or interactive modules that a child completes on their own schedule (covering concepts like “how to organize your study space” or “voice modulation techniques”). Then once a week, they attend a live coaching session to practice those concepts, ask questions, and get feedback. This format allows more efficient use of live time (focus on practice rather than lecturing content) and flexibility (if a child misses a concept, the async lesson is there to review). It’s akin to “flipped classroom” model. It also allows a more scalable product – one coach can handle more students if they are primarily guiding practice and discussion, not teaching basics repeatedly. So, for a startup, this could mean developing a library of high-quality micro-lessons (maybe gamified or interactive to keep engagement) and having coaches facilitate group or 1:1 sessions applying those lessons. It could particularly work for study skills (lots of content like techniques can be taught via short videos, then students come together to share how it worked for them, troubleshoot with the coach) or even for communication (videos demonstrate techniques, then live sessions for delivering speeches). Currently, pure-play soft skills startups haven’t done much async content – presumably to maintain human touch. But that leaves an efficiency opportunity if done right. This approach could also bring cost down for customers (less live hours to pay for) or increase margin. It would differentiate by offering a more flexible learning experience and possibly a tech angle (the platform with micro-lessons).

School-Integrated Programs (with Teacher Training & Curriculum Plug-ins): Almost all current companies go direct to consumer. Yet, a huge opportunity is to work through schools – many schools know soft skills are important but lack resources or curricula to teach them well. A venture could create a program that integrates into the school day or after-school clubs. This would involve providing training or co-facilitators to school teachers, plus curriculum materials, perhaps an online portal for activities. The model might be B2B2C: sell licenses to schools or districts to implement a soft skills curriculum, and possibly supplement with expert-led sessions or online components. For instance, a “Study Skills across the Curriculum” package that a school buys – it trains teachers to incorporate study strategy lessons in class, provides an app for students to track habits, and includes monthly online workshops with an external expert for students/parents. Or a “Soft Skills Lab” that a school runs weekly where content is provided by the company (videos, lesson plans) and maybe remote mentors join occasionally. This format leverages schools’ captive audience and can reach those who wouldn’t enroll in a private program. It also aligns with increasing emphasis by education boards on SEL and 21st-century skills. It is underused because selling to schools can be slow and many startups avoid it initially. But it’s promising if one can demonstrate efficacy because schools have scale and funding (especially post-pandemic funds for learning loss often include SEL and skills recovery). Additionally, integrating with schools adds credibility to a program and can be a differentiator (e.g., “our program is implemented in XYZ top schools”). It could also include teacher training as a component – empowering teachers to reinforce soft skills in their teaching. None of our profiled companies heavily pursues B2B school integration (PlanetSpark and others focus on B2C). That leaves an opportunity for someone to become the go-to curriculum provider for soft skills in schools, akin to how some companies provide STEM curriculum to schools.

(4) Differentiation Opportunities:

Specific Outcome Focus (“Nailing a Niche Outcome”): A venture can differentiate by being the best at solving one clear problem that others only address broadly. For example:

“Study Skills for ADHD/ADD Learners” – Market explicitly as the program that understands ADHD brains and improves their school performance by teaching tailored strategies (frequent breaks, externalizing reminders, etc.). This specificity can attract a devoted segment and face little direct competition besides individual ADHD coaches.

“Soft Skills for Young Founders/Entrepreneurs” – A niche for teens interested in entrepreneurship: teach leadership, pitching, networking, creativity, and resilience in a startup context. Possibly culminating in a project (like a mini business or social enterprise). There are a few programs like Junior Achievement or incubator competitions for teens, but an online cohort that trains entrepreneurial soft skills as an integrated program is rare. It would attract ambitious teens and signal to parents as something beyond typical debate or coding clubs.

“Academic Resilience for High-Pressure Exam Systems” – As mentioned, targeting students in rigorous tracks (like Dutch VWO/Gymnasium, British GCSE/A-Levels, IB Diploma, etc.). These students face intense exam stress and often there’s a gap in support. A program here could combine study techniques, stress management, and growth mindset specifically framed for surviving and thriving in those exam systems. For example, in the Netherlands, VWO and Gymnasium students might benefit from a course on handling the independent study load and exam stress unique to that system (language localized). No one is branding to this specifically; it’s usually generic tutoring or nothing.

Essentially, by identifying a painful, specific outcome and becoming synonymous with solving it, a startup can avoid being a me-too. It can still cover multiple skills, but all in service of that outcome (e.g., teaching time management, note-taking, and emotional regulation all to help ADHD kids succeed – that’s the narrative unifier).

Depth of Pedagogy (Backed by Science or Frameworks): Many current programs use generic best practices, but few heavily market their scientific basis. There’s room to differentiate with evidence-based methods:

For instance, a program built on cognitive science (perhaps in partnership with a university) that uses proven techniques like spaced learning (for study habits) or cognitive-behavioral techniques (for anxiety in speaking). If one can claim “our curriculum is developed with psychologists from XYZ and shown to improve confidence by X%” and back it up with data, that’s powerful.

Or using established youth development frameworks (like the 4Cs model or CASEL’s SEL framework) but in a new engaging delivery. HOSS does mention AI-supported personalized c, hinting at innovation, but not much detail publicly. A startup could emphasize a “research-backed curriculum that adapts to your child” – for example, initial assessments to tailor the focus (if a child already confident but disorganized, focus on EF, if articulate but not empathetic, focus on EQ).

**Another angle:** incorporate coaching frameworks used in executive coaching but simplified for kids (like GROW model for goal-setting, etc.). This can raise perceived quality – akin to giving kids the kind of personal development usually reserved for executives.

Being able to demonstrate measurable progress scientifically (like using pre/post skill assessments or validated scales of self-confidence or habits) can also differentiate from anecdote-driven competitors. A platform might include a baseline “soft skills assessment” at start and end (some SEL assessments exist) to show growth numerically, appealing to results-oriented parents.

**Superior Measurement of Progress:** Tied to pedagogy, but specifically, progress tracking is an area for innovation. PlanetSpark introduced some AI progress reports, but generally parents rely on seeing a child perform or a certificate. A venture that offers a dashboard or report card for soft skills could stand out. For instance, if after each session or week, parents get a report: “This week, Alex increased their speaking time from 1 minute to 2 minutes, maintained eye contact 70% of the time (up from 50%), and showed improvement in using vocal variation. Here’s a video clip comparison.” That level of detail would impress parents. Or for study skills: “Mia used the planner 5 out of 5 days and completed tasks 90% on time, up from 50% last month.” Achieving this might involve tech tools (perhaps having kids log activities, or use AI to analyze speech or facial expressions for confidence markers). If done well, it not only proves impact but also engages parents and students (makes progress tangible like a game).

Additionally, including parents in feedback loops (maybe monthly coach-parent meetings to discuss progress, or sending tips based on child’s data) could set a new standard for involving stakeholders.

Essentially, become known as the data-driven soft skills program in a field that’s traditionally fuzzy. It’s tricky to quantify some soft outcomes, but even proxies or subjective ratings can be presented professionally. This could also feed into testimonials (“95% of our students show a 20% increase in our communication score within 3 months” – having that kind of stat builds trust).

Language/Localization for Europe and LatAm: As identified, localizing content and context is a big differentiator. For example:

**A program that is Dutch-first:** It uses Dutch language role-plays, addresses skills needed in Dutch schools (like how to do spreekbeurt – a common Dutch class presentation assignment – confidently, or how to handle group work the Dutch way), and maybe integrates aspects of Dutch culture (direct communication style, etc.). Parents in the Netherlands might prefer this to a generic English program because it feels more relevant and also because younger kids might not be fluent in English yet.

**Similarly for Latin America:** a Spanish program that references scenarios Hispanic kids relate to, perhaps using culturally relevant storytelling or addressing issues common in Latin schooling (like dealing with very authoritarian teaching styles, or boosting creativity in rote environments).

Even within Europe, catering to languages like German, French, Spanish, Italian – whichever has a gap. It’s not easy to cover all languages, so picking one or two to specialize in can capture those markets. PlanetSpark and others haven’t moved beyond English (and Russian in KidsKey’s unique case). This leaves a huge segment open – many parents will choose an offering in their native language for their kids over an English one, especially if the kids are younger or not in bilingual schools.

Localization also means understanding local educational systems and values. For instance, Germany has a strong tradition of clubs (Vereine) for skills like debate or theatre, but maybe less in schools. A program might tie into that structure. Or in Japan/South Korea, there’s intense focus on exams and less on soft skills traditionally, but now they realize creativity/communication is needed – a localized program in Japanese or Korean that supplements could do well (though those markets have language barriers and local companies likely to appear).

The opportunity is a “Blue Ocean” in each non-English market – while competition rages in English, one could become the top brand in Spanish or French soft skills training before others translate their content.

In combining these differentiation points, a powerful strategy might be: choose a specific underserved segment/problem, design a program with science-backed depth for that niche, measure outcomes rigorously, and deliver it in an innovative format that competitors haven’t tried. Add a dash of localization if regionally focused.
For example, imagine a startup: FocusMinds – an online coaching program for middle-schoolers with attention issues (underserved persona), uses evidence-based techniques from psychology (depth), tracks metrics like task completion and attention spans via an app (measurement), delivered as a long-term small cohort with the same coach (format), and initially targeting English-speaking but could localize. That checks many boxes above and would stand out distinctly among broad “public speaking for kids” offerings.
**Another example:** EuroSoft (placeholder name) – offering soft skills classes in multiple European languages with cultural context (localization), partnering with schools (format), focusing on 21st century skills integration in local curricula (specific niche: EU key competences perhaps), using a consistent framework to measure student skill improvement (measurement). This would fill the gap in Europe where currently parents either use English programs or nothing.The key is that the market is not one-size – by identifying a slice that others have overlooked or not served well, a new venture can gain traction without directly head-to-head against heavily funded players in their stronghold.
# Go-to-Market and Feasibility Questions for a New Venture
Finally, tying the research back to planning a potential startup, here are critical questions and strategic considerations:
Q1. Which specific learner segment and problem will I focus on first, and why is it both painful and currently poorly served?
**Analysis:** The research suggests many potential segments (e.g., ADHD teens lacking study skills, or Spanish-speaking tweens needing confidence). I should pinpoint one where the pain point is acute (parents are actively seeking a solution or willing to pay if one existed) and current options are few or inadequate. For instance, if I choose “executive function skills for middle schoolers with ADHD,” the pain is clear: these kids struggle daily with organization, grades suffer, family stress is high – and existing help might only be expensive 1:1 coaches or generic advice. That justifies a focused solution. Alternatively, if I choose a regional focus like “soft skills for Latin American teens in Spanish,” the pain might be that parents know their kids need these for a globalized future but have no accessible programs in Spanish – a strong gap. I need to articulate why my chosen niche has high unmet demand (perhaps through some user interviews or observing online forums of parents discussing these struggles). This will guide the entire product design and marketing messaging. Starting niche does not mean small impact if the pain point is widespread in that group (e.g., ADHD is common enough, and Latin America is huge enough). So the question to answer: Where can I deliver a knockout solution that others aren’t addressing yet? – The above research gave many ideas. Now I must validate one by checking interest (e.g., survey parents, gauge search volumes) and ensure these customers can be reached.Q2. What is my wedge product – the simplest, most focused first offering – that delivers a clear, measurable outcome within 4–8 weeks?
**Analysis:** In a world of long curricula, a crisp initial product that quickly proves value can attract customers and build trust. The research shows parents like trial classes and short courses to test waters. So, I should design a short program (maybe 6–8 sessions over 6–8 weeks) with a very tangible result. For example, if my niche is study skills for 13-year-olds, my wedge product might be a “1-Month Study Skills Bootcamp: Get Organized and Ace Your Next Test”. Outcome: child goes from messy backpack and last-minute cramming to using a planner and feeling prepared for an upcoming exam – something the parent can observe (the next test grade might even improve). Or if I focus on public speaking for shy kids as niche: a “8-week Confidence Kickstarter – from Silent to Speaking Up in Class.” Outcome: child raises hand in class or gives a 2-minute presentation by the end, recorded as proof. This wedge product should be laser-focused on one goal, not trying to teach everything. It serves as a proof of concept and a marketing hook. It should also be relatively easy to deliver (I can fine-tune curriculum for one specific goal). After they complete it and see success, I can then upsell into longer or broader programs. The key from research: promises must be credible and outcomes visible (PlanetSpark’s demo for instance gives detailed feedback, which is like a micro outcome). So I’d define success metrics (e.g., a before/after self-assessment, or parental feedback like “he now does homework without being told 3 times”). The wedge also sets my brand’s reputation, so picking an outcome that I can consistently achieve with good methodology (backed by the science from earlier) is crucial.Q3. How can I design a learning experience that kids actually enjoy and want to continue, parents perceive as clearly valuable, and that can scale beyond a few tutors?
**Analysis:** Balancing engagement, parent satisfaction, and scalability is the crux of feasibility:

**Kid Enjoyment:** From research, fun and interactivity are mandatory. Techniques: gamification (points, levels, small rewards like PlanetSpark’s b), use of themes (like incorporating topics kids love, whether it’s Minecraft analogies for study skills or fun challenges in public speaking classes), social aspects (kids tend to enjoy making friends in class; the long-term cohort idea leverages that). Also, not overloading them – short sessions, variety of activities to suit short attention spans. Maybe a bit of competition or showcase (kids love showing off a project or speech if they are prepared). I must consider age-appropriate methods; what’s fun for a 9-year-old might bore a 16-year-old. So tailoring content style per age group is key. Possibly involve game elements like an online platform where they complete quests (e.g., each week’s tasks are quests).

**Parent Perceived Value:** Parents want to see progress or at least positive change in behavior/attitude. So I will incorporate parent updates (like weekly summary emails: “This week we learned X, your child did Y, try asking them about Z.”). Also maybe involve them occasionally (point from earlier) so they witness the learning. Providing something tangible – could be a certificate, or better a final presentation or project that parents can see (like a video of their child speaking, or a before-after planner picture). Testimonials and results from other kids (once I have them) will reassure that value is delivered. Perhaps even a small pre-post assessment that parents see (“initial confidence score 6/10, after course 8/10 as rated by our rubric”). The key is transparency: let parents know exactly what skills are being built and show evidence. Value also equals convenience and support: flexible scheduling, recordings if a class is missed, accessible coaches for questions (like office hours or a chat).

**Scalability beyond a few tutors:** This is critical if I want to grow. The program cannot hinge on one charismatic instructor (which is a problem for some classes on Outschool – only that teacher can draw crowds). I should codify the curriculum (lesson plans, materials, activities) so that I can train new coaches to deliver it with similar quality. Possibly create a coach training program (like HOSS mentions training t). Standardize the approach (e.g., use the same platform tools, same games, same feedback forms) to reduce variance. Also, leveraging some technology helps scale: if I use asynchronous modules or AI-driven feedback for part of it, I’m less reliant on high teacher-student ratios. For example, kids could practice a speech on an AI tool that gives initial feedback on filler words or pace; then a coach just reviews highlights – thus one coach can handle more kids. Community or peer-led elements (like buddies or peer feedback sessions) can also reduce load on instructors and let me scale class sizes moderately while maintaining quality (e.g., in a group of 8, peers give feedback first, then coach adds). Another aspect is hiring: perhaps tapping into a pipeline of talented coaches (education students, psychologists, former teachers) and giving them a motivating reason to work with me (flexible work, good pay per hour, training). If my content is well-structured and I have a mentorship system (senior coach supervises new ones), I can ensure quality at scale. Also, an online platform to manage scheduling, feedback and progress tracking will be needed to handle many classes and personalized reporting at scale (maybe starting with off-the-shelf LMS or even spreadsheets, but planning for a custom solution if scaling up).

Summarizing, I'll likely design a blended approach: engaging live sessions plus interactive content (so kids enjoy), robust parent communication and visible progress (so parents value it), and a standardized curriculum with tech support (to scale). For example, a weekly format where Monday kids watch a short fun video and do a quick game, Wednesday they join a live small group with a trained coach following a script/plan, and continuous use of an app to practice daily (scalable practice). That synergy can check all boxes.
Q4. What combination of content (curriculum), coaching (live interaction), community (peers, parents), and tools (planners, habit trackers, AI helpers) would create a defensible and sticky product?
**Analysis:** This question pushes me to integrate multiple elements to increase the product’s value and defensibility (i.e., not easily copied).

Content/Curriculum: I’ll need to develop proprietary content that’s effective and engaging – like a series of modules, activities, and lessons that are uniquely mine (perhaps using a theme or method I create, e.g., “Superhero Study System” or “Communication Champions Quest”, something branded). The curriculum should ideally be adaptable to individuals (personalized pathways if possible). It should also draw on best practices (so it gets results, making it defensible via efficacy).

**Coaching:** The human element is crucial for soft skills. My coaches/mentors are part of the IP if they are trained in a specific methodology. A strong coach-student relationship can make it sticky (kids stay because they like their mentor). Perhaps I can incorporate a bit of one-on-one coaching even if main sessions are group – e.g., a monthly 15-minute 1:1 check-in for each kid. That personal touch is both valuable (parents love personalized attention) and somewhat defensible (it’s not just content someone can replicate, it’s a relationship). However, reliance solely on star coaches is a risk, so I want a framework that any good educator can follow and still deliver effectively – again training and materials matter.

Community (Peers & Parents): Creating a peer community adds stickiness: if kids form friendships or at least enjoy interacting with the same group, they’ll want to continue (like how Outschool clubs retained kids for over a y). I might incorporate group projects or a collaborative forum (maybe between sessions, kids share something on a moderated chat or group board). Could even extend community beyond class: alumni groups, events (maybe an online speech competition, or a virtual showcase to which multiple cohorts come together). For parents, a community could be a value-add: perhaps a parent Facebook or WhatsApp group where tips are shared and they see other parents also care about these issues (peer validation). If I facilitate parent webinars or Q&A sessions (like “office hours for parents on how to support study habits”), that creates loyalty because the parent feels supported too. A strong parent community can drive referrals (they'll tell friends if they feel part of something meaningful).

Tools (Planners, Trackers, AI, etc.): This is a big differentiator potential. For study skills, providing a custom planner or a habit tracking app for kids, integrated with the program, is great. For example, if I give each student a cool physical or digital planner and teach them to use it, that tool becomes part of daily life. A digital habit tracker app (maybe I create a simple one or use something like Trello for kids) could be used to track goals or tasks, and coaches can see the data to give feedback. AI can be leveraged for things like analyzing speeches (tone, pace) or analyzing writing for creativity, etc., providing additional feedback that scales. If I incorporate an AI “buddy” that prompts the child through tasks (e.g., a chatbot reminding them of their goals or offering tips when they struggle), that’s innovative and sticky. Tools also create switching costs: if all a child’s plans and progress are on my app, leaving the program means losing that structure. Similarly, giving something like a personalized report or portfolio (e.g., a digital portfolio of speeches, projects, badges earned) becomes a record they want to keep building.

In combination, the synergy might be: a structured curriculum delivered by nurturing coaches, enriched by a supportive peer network and engaged parents, all facilitated through tech tools that reinforce behaviors and track progress. This integrated ecosystem is hard to replicate by a single competitor easily (some might have good curriculum but no tech, or tech but no community, etc.).

For defensibility, a data advantage could emerge: if my tools collect unique data on how kids improve (e.g., thousands of data points on habit formation or speech improvement), I could refine my AI or curriculum better than newcomers, making the product more effective over time. Also, a multi-faceted product is harder to copy than a single course – a competitor might imitate one piece (like similar lesson plans), but copying an engaged community or a trove of user data for AI is much tougher.

Stickiness (customer retention) will come from making the experience feel like more than a class – rather a journey or membership. If kids make friends and have an identity (like “I’m a Level 2 Confidence Cadet in this program, aiming to be a Communication Captain”), they have an incentive to continue. If parents see continuous improvement and feel part of the solution, they’ll re-enroll. Tools like trackers create daily/weekly engagement beyond the live class – increasing stickiness by embedding into routine.

I should also consider gamified progression (badges, levels, unlocking new content) to motivate long-term participation. Maybe after the initial 8-week wedge, there’s a longer-term pathway (like HOSS’s Gold/Silver/Platinum l). That gives families a reason to stick around – aspiring to reach the next level, which implies continued subscription.

Q5. Which initial channels will I use to test demand cheaply: local schools/tutoring centers, small online cohorts via social media, partnerships with existing edtechs to plug in a module?
**Analysis:** Before scaling, I need to validate my concept with minimal cost:

**Local Schools or Tutoring Centers:** If I am focusing on a certain age or academic context (e.g., IB students), I could approach a couple of local schools or centers to run a pilot workshop for free or low cost. For instance, offer a 2-session mini-program on study skills to a class or an after-school group, gather feedback and outcomes. **Small Online Cohorts via Social Media Recruitment:** I can directly target my segment through social media groups. For example, if I choose ADHD study skills, I know there are Facebook groups or forums for parents of ADHD kids, subreddit communities, etc. I could post educational content or an invite like “seeking a few middle-schoolers for a free pilot program on study habits – ADHD friendly!” This kind of outreach can gather a small group quickly if the pain point resonates (parents are often eager for help). Even without ads, organic participation can happen in niche communities. Alternatively, create a simple landing page and share it in relevant social groups, or use my network (ask friends who are parents or teachers if they know kids who would try it). Running a small cohort on Zoom gives me direct evidence of engagement and outcomes. It’s also easier to iterate in a small setting. The key is to be where my target parents congregate online – could be FB, Insta (maybe parent influencers), or local WhatsApp parent networks. If focusing regionally, for example Latin America, maybe use WhatsApp or local parent blogs.

**Partnerships with Existing Edtechs:** This is an interesting route – essentially piggybacking on someone else’s user base initially. Perhaps I could approach a platform like Outschool to list a pilot class (leveraging their user base to test interest). Or find a complementary edtech: e.g., a tutoring company might let me run a soft skills workshop for their students as a value-add, or a homeschool curriculum site might feature my mini-course. The benefit is those companies have distribution; the challenge is aligning incentives (edtech may want a cut or their brand first). If I put up “Executive Function Skills for Teens – 4 week course” and it gets enrollments, that’s a validation. Outschool does marketing to parents who search on their site, so if I get traction there, it shows genuine demand. **Local Outreach:** if targeting local region (like Dutch market), I could try a local approach – giving a talk at a library or community center about study skills, then offering a sign-up for a trial course. Traditional but effective on small scale and free aside from time.

Essentially, the plan is to start with low-cost experiments: create a minimal version of the product and find a few customers through direct outreach or piggybacking. For each channel:
