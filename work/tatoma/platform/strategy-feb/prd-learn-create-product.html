Who Is This For

Three Employee Mindsets

Every organization has the same distribution. Learn & Create needs to serve all three — with different entry points for each.

40%

The Curious

"I hear AI can help with my work, but I don't know how it applies to what I do."

Interested but overwhelmed. Needs a structured starting point. The Academy is their entry.

35%

The Skeptic

"I asked ChatGPT to write an email and it sounded robotic. AI isn't for me."

Tried it, got burned. Needs a quick win to change their mind. Cards are their entry — instant value, no training required.

25%

The Early Adopter

"I've got my own prompts in a notes app. Works for me."

Already using AI but ad-hoc. Needs structure and sharing. The prompt library and use case generator are their entry.

What they all share: They need AI to solve their problems, not abstract corporate ones. The entry point is personal productivity, not transformation strategy.

Why Corporate AI Training Fails

Most organizations follow the same broken pattern — and get the same result.

The Typical Pattern

1

Leadership announces "We're going all-in on AI"

2

A one-day workshop happens — inspiring, occasionally hands-on

3

Everyone goes back to their desks and nothing changes

4

Six months later: "Why isn't anyone using AI?"

The fundamental problem: training is an event, not a system.

✓

✓

What Section (Prof AI) got right — and where they stop

Section pivoted to product-led AI training. They understand self-service, personal use cases, certs, and consumption pricing. But they don't connect training to transformation. There's no link between "I discovered a use case" and "this feeds into the company's AI roadmap." That's Tatoma's edge: the two products share a brain.

How We Design

Design Principles

Five rules that shape every design and content decision in Learn & Create.

1

Personal before organizational

Nobody cares about "AI transformation." They care about leaving work 30 minutes earlier. Start from personal value.

2

Show, don't teach

The fastest path to adoption isn't reading about AI — it's doing something with AI. Every module has a hands-on exercise. Cards provide instant value.

3

Lower the floor, raise the ceiling

Beginners feel confident starting. Advanced users find depth in the prompt refiner and custom use cases. Neither path feels condescending.

4

Product, not training software

No "mandatory training" vibes. No corporate language. Clean design, fast interactions. Think Duolingo, not Cornerstone.

5

Everything feeds up

Individual discoveries aggregate into organizational intelligence. Use case counts, skill levels, adoption metrics — all visible without employees doing extra work.

What Matters Most

Three Things to Nail at Launch

The first version doesn't need every feature. It needs these three things to be excellent.

Must nail

Academy content quality

If AI Foundations feels like a corporate slide deck, we've lost. It must be conversational, practical, and end with genuine "aha" moments. Content quality, not feature quantity.

Must nail

Cards as instant value

Cards are the "try before you learn" entry point. A skeptic will try the Email Writer card. If it saves them time, they'll explore more. Cards win skeptics.

Must nail

Use cases feel personal

"What can AI do for HR?" is interesting. "What can AI do for you, a benefits coordinator who spends 3h/week on enrollment questions?" is compelling.

What can wait

Q3

Department-specific paths

Get the universal AI Foundations path right first. Department content multiplies the authoring burden.

Q3

LMS integration

Nice for enterprise sales, not needed for product validation. Design schema to support it.

Later

Cross-client content sharing

Anonymized use case library across all clients. Valuable at scale, premature with 3 clients.

Later

Gamification

Can drive engagement, but substance matters more first. Don't mask mediocre content with points.

What Could Go Wrong

Risks & Mitigations

High

"Nobody completes the training"

Corporate training has ~15% completion rate industry-wide.

Mitigation: Make the first 3 modules irresistible. End each with a hands-on win. Keep modules at 10-15 min. Use Cards as the "instant gratification" alternative.
High

"The content isn't good enough"

One shot at first impressions. Too basic or too technical both fail.

Mitigation: Every module must answer "what can I do differently tomorrow?" Co-founders write the first path. Test with real employees before launch.
High

"One developer can't build both products"

Transformation OS is 12 weeks. Learn & Create is another 12.

Mitigation: Sequence: Transformation OS first (Q2), Learn & Create second (Q3). OR: build L&C Phase A in parallel with Transformation OS Phase C-D (independent codebases).
Medium

"It feels like corporate training software"

LMS vibes, mandatory tone, slow loading — kills engagement before it starts.

Mitigation: Consumer-grade UX. No "required training" language. Fast, clean, Tatoma-branded. Think Duolingo, not Moodle.
Medium

"AI inference costs explode at 1000 users"

Use case generator, prompt refiner, and Cards all call AI APIs at 100x user scale.

Mitigation: Use Groq (cheapest inference). Rate-limit per user. Cache common templates. Monitor cost per client weekly.
For Tomorrow

What to Discuss

Six decisions that shape how and when Learn & Create comes to life.

1

Sequencing

Transformation OS first, then Learn & Create? Or parallel? The strategy session called L&C "the most urgent build." One developer makes parallel risky. What's the real priority?

2

Minimum viable Academy

6-8 modules for AI Foundations is the plan — but that's 40+ hours of content work. Can we launch with 4 and expand? Who writes them?

3

Cards as early access

Cards provide instant value and don't require content creation. Should we make them available before the Academy is ready — as the "early access" version of Learn & Create?

4

Pricing model

Included in base subscription (drives stickiness) or priced separately (clear standalone value, usage-based costs)? Per-user above a threshold?

5

Content authoring investment

Is there commitment for ~40 hours of initial content work across the three co-founders? (Outlines + review of AI-expanded drafts)

6

Pilot scope

All employees (risky at scale)? One department (safer, less data)? Volunteer group (self-selecting bias)?
